Originally Answered: How do you make a cryptocurrency?

Here is a really, really, REALLY condensed answer to your question. Just the key bullet points:

1. I suggest that you be or partner with a good coder.

2. Then, study Satoshi's original white paper.

3. Next, decide if you will be creating a Bitcoin fork, or something completely different…

  • 3a. Option A: Bitcoin Fork

If you want to emulate a massively popular currency, perhaps tweaking it to be more efficient, adding features or addressing shortcomings, then download and begin studying the Bitcoin core. It is completely open source and freely available to copy. You can create an “Elijah Coin” in just a day—and then begin adding improvements.

But beware! If you keep any part of your mechanism proprietary (secret), then no one will use your coin. It must be open source and free to copy or modify. For this reason, Bitcoin, itself, may freely fold-in your features and improvements. And they already have achieved a global supply/demand network. This tough truism will force you to assess your motives and goals.

  • 3b. Option B: Not a Bitcoin

If you want to create a unique digital coin, or some type of a voting/contract mechanism,* it is a good bet that you should still base it on a blockchain architecture (distributed, crowd-source ledger, with mining rewards to grow the validation pool).

Why? Because the blockchain is the most transformative development in trust and validation since the emergence of the global Internet—or at least since the advent of RSA public-key encryption. Unless you have the inspiration and chutspah to eclipse Staoshi’s child, consider building your new coin on a foundation that is pre-qualified, because it has already built a winner.

* Ethereum is not a currency. It addresses a different market than Bitcoin. But still, it is built on blockchain technology.

4. Next, decide some basic things about your starting conditions and supply cap…

  • Do you want to pocket the first 10% of all wealth. (Hint: Don’t do it. Doing this will fail to generate trust and adoption. Avoid setting yourself up to be any more important or entitled than any other user).
  • Do you want to tie an initial corpus to existing Bitcoin ownership (Hint: Do this or you will fail. Mastercoin did this, and it is likely the reason that it is a viable contender for representing stored value).

5. Promote your new baby.

Satoshi was first, he was brilliant and he was lucky. Market timing was impeccable. You no longer have any of these things going for you. In fact, you must punch open a window of opportunity. So, you will need three things to divert attention to your project:

  • A pithy, catchy and crystal clear elevator pitch
  • It must be supremely compelling
  • Therefore, it must convey high value and low risk

Don’t expect the world to understand your new currency. Pundits are brutal. Viral adoption is a long shot. Therefore, a critical component of your pitch is the adoption plan. Throughout the adoption plan, reinforce your explanation of the user benefit.

Differentiate. Why does anyone need your cryptocurrency? Why is it better than others? Make the value seem obvious. Explain barriers to entry.

Speaking of adoption, you will need financial backers (at least USD $2M)—or lots and lots of TV and magazine editors in your back pocket. Start cultivating them during the early phase, but size them up early. Editors who don’t quite get the value proposition will hurt you more than they help you. (Walt Mossberg of the Wall Street Journal almost killed a venture of mine because of a subtle misunderstanding).

That, in a nutshell, is my action plan for launching your new cryptocurrency. Good luck, Elijah!

Originally Answered: How do you make a cryptocurrency?
A cryptocurrency is a solution to the problem of verifying the order of events without a central database, i.e. distributed consensus, i.e. Byzantine fault tolerance.

The question is, essentially, if no node on the network can be trusted on its own, how to make it so you can trust the network as a whole. In this case the solution is to make people expend lots of computing resources on searching for a solution to an arbitrary, but specifically-tailored problem (has adjustable difficulty, which allows the creation of scarcity - when more resources join the network, just up the difficulty to even-out the generation rate).

The idea is that you trust the chain (here's where "blockchain" comes from) that is longest, i.e. that has had the most resources poured into it - if somebody is trying to manipulate transactions, they'll have to have at least 51% of the total resources of the network (which is currently the most powerful "supercomputer" in the world, by a HUGE margin).

Transactions in already confirmed blocks cannot be altered without redoing ALL the work since the transaction, and successfully outracing the miners that are already ahead (the blocks are made so each one depends on the previous - if you change one, you have to change all the others after it).

The language you write it in is pretty much irrelevant, the problem is that this relies heavily on network effects. You can create a currency in a lazy afternoon if you just clone Bitcoin (hey, it's open source!), but if nobody uses it except you, its value is 0 (except if you provide some kind of unique service... are you an amusement park by a chance? I hear Disney World prints their own currency).

However, I think this problem is solved by sidechains (A simple explanation of Bitcoin “Sidechains”), which allow you to peg your own fork to Bitcoin. Thus you are free to change it up, while inheriting the value from Bitcoin (because your currency will be easily convertible to/from BTC).

P.S. The function they're solving is rounds of SHA-256 hashes, just increase a counter in the block until you get a hash that starts with a certain number of zero digits. Less zeros = lower difficulty, more zeros = higher difficulty. You include a certain amount of reward Bitcoins in the transaction, so when you find the solution, you send it to the network, which can easily verify your solution is correct (by just using the same number you did, and getting the same hash).

What does it take to make a Cryptocurrency?

You better sit down and grab a cup of coffee for this one.

First of all, before you consider launching your own token/coin, ask yourself - is it necessary?

Can you really "tokenize" some type of value you plan to deliver?

Many people forget that ICO's and Token Sales are merely a form of crowd funding that instead of requesting FIAT currency, are asking for purchases of their tokens/coins, hoping that not only will they raise the necessary funds to accomplish their goals - but also that the tokens/coins will appreciate over time if they do in-fact deliver value over time, and continue to grow as a company.

So, as many have already alluded to - there are a handful of things that are imperative, these include:

#1 A developer who understands Blockchain technology, as well as the platform you intend to build on (Bitcoin, Ethereum, or perhaps another).

#2 Go check out Ethereum Project, or the original Whitepaper by Satoshi can also be found below.

#3 Determine if you're going to make a 'fork' off an existing popular currency, or make a completely new token simply utilizing the platform itself.

Depending on the route you decide to go with will ultimately determine what type of company you become, and how people will perceive your company. A fork could make sense if you strongly feel that you can improve upon the existing technology. Otherwise, it makes more sense to create a token simply utilizing the technology to build upon.

#4 Either way, after that you need to determine how many tokens/coins you're going to make available for purchase. This is pretty important, because there are a lot of companies making supply caps that are literally in the trillions - I laugh while I write this, because I think about how silly this is, and essentially no different than creating a penny stock at that point. If that's what you're looking to do, you may as well save yourself the effort of going through the ICO process.

#5 Marketing. A good company I've found that I know has been helpful promoting other successful ICO's is BrandBuilders - you can check out their website, they've been around for a while and typically work with corporate clients, Startups, and different types of businesses but also develop their own internal SAAS products they offer to consumers as well.

The thing is, even if you have the best product in the world - if you don't tell anyone about it, no one will ever know.

If you’re serious about it then it’s a serious pain. Basically you have to be:

  1. An expert programmer.
  2. An expert programmer who’s also an expert in distributed p2p network systems.
  3. An expert programmer who’s also an expert (or at least familiar enough with to adapt an off-the-shelf solution) in cryptography. (which is required even if the network’s security doesn’t depend on it, because the currency addresses do)
  4. An expert programmer who is also familiar with all of the various distributed consensus algorithms available. (blockchain, tangle, hashgraph, PBFT, ???)
  5. Preferably an expert at economics. Cryptocurrency is fundamentally currency, and in order to design one well you need to ensure that it’s qualities as a currency are as good as possible. You also need to consider how to introduce your currency in order to get the best possible economic outcome (which is waaay harder than you think it is).

On the other hand just making another bitcoin clone isn’t quite as hard, although the programming aspects are still ridiculous. The downside to that is that you’ll be looking at an oversaturated market that’s already dominated by bitcoin and ethereum, and which is already plagued by the serious scaling issues inherent in blockchain technology.

If you’re just looking to scam people out of their money it’s probably easier to create a startup with “blockchain” in the name, or which otherwise advertises that it “leverages” blockchain technology. Then you won’t have to do any actual work.

Originally Answered: How do you make a cryptocurrency?
Its almost certainly not worthwhile to create a new cryptocurrency unless you are solving some very serious limitation in Bitcoin. If you look at the volumes and captializations (Crypto-Currency Market Capitalizations) of the various cryptocurrencies, essentially none of them except Bitcoin, Litecoin and Ethereum are alive. At different times, various others (Paycoin, Dogecoin) have briefly had volume, but they have collapsed. In fact, most new cryptocurrencies are little more than get-rich-quick schemes for the people who launch them.

If you want to experiment with creating a cryptocurrency and have limited experience with programming and cryptography, look first at whether what you want to do can be done on top of Ethereum, which is platform on which you can build any application that uses the blockchain.

Creating a cryptocurrency from scratch is a large software engineering project that requires cryptography expertise. Most "new" cryptocurrencies are in fact clones of Bitcoin's source code with some changes implemented on top. One or two (Eg. Ethereum) are rewrites. Even if you clone Bitcoin, this isn't at all a simple project. Even simply testing and verifying protocol changes requires you to set up a test environment which itself is not trivial. 

The programming language is irrelevant. As it happens, Bitcoin and Litecoin are currently written in C. Ethereum is written in Golang.
